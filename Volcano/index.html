Stratovolcano Risk in the 21st Century

A global analysis of volcano distribution, volcano types, and country-level concentration of significant eruptions, 2000–2025

By Yichen Li

Final Project, 2026

Significant eruptions are not spread evenly across the world. They cluster most heavily along tectonic boundaries, with especially strong concentration around the Pacific Ring of Fire. This pattern shows that volcanic risk is structured geographically rather than randomly distributed. At the global level, the Pacific stands out as the central zone of significant volcanic activity, making it the clearest starting point for understanding where dangerous eruptions are most likely to occur. This project focuses specifically on significant eruptions. In this dataset, a significant eruption is defined as one that caused deaths, caused notable damage, reached a Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) of 6 or higher, triggered a tsunami, or was associated with a major earthquake.

This project narrows from a broad global view to a more specific argument. Volcanoes are split into five volcano types: stratovolcano, complex volcano, caldera, shield volcano, and other. I begin with the worldwide distribution of volcanoes, then focus on stratovolcanoes as the most consequential type in the dataset, due to their combination of frequent eruptions and high human impact, and finally look at the countries where significant stratovolcano eruptions are most concentrated. Taken together, these three views move from global context to type-level importance and then to country-level concentration.

Among the five volcano types in this dataset, stratovolcanoes stand out most clearly. They account for the largest number of significant eruptions and also show the highest average deaths per eruption. This combination of frequency and lethality makes them more consequential than the other volcano types considered here. In this dataset, stratovolcanoes are not only common, but also especially destructive in human terms.

The risks associated with stratovolcanoes are also unevenly distributed across countries. Between 2000 and 2025, Indonesia recorded the highest cumulative number of significant stratovolcano eruptions among the five countries shown here. This suggests that the burden of dangerous stratovolcano activity is concentrated in particular national contexts rather than shared equally across all volcanic regions. Indonesia stands out most clearly as the country where this concentration is strongest.

Conclusion

Significant volcanic risk is not evenly distributed across space, volcano types, or countries. Instead, it becomes more concentrated at each level of the analysis: first around the Pacific, then in stratovolcanoes, and finally in Indonesia. This pattern suggests that the danger associated with volcanic eruptions is not just a matter of where volcanoes exist, but of which volcanoes erupt, how destructive they are, and where that destruction is most concentrated. Taken together, these results show why stratovolcanoes deserve particular attention in any discussion of significant eruption risk in the 21st century.
